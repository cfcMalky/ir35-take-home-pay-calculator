IR35 — the essentials

What is IR35 and why does it matter to contractors?

IR35 is HMRC legislation designed to prevent "disguised employment" — where someone works like an employee but bills through a limited company to pay less tax. If your contract is deemed inside IR35, you're taxed as if you were an employee of the end client, even though you're not.

The practical impact is significant. Inside IR35, you lose the ability to take dividends (which are taxed more favourably than salary), you pay employer's National Insurance on top of your day rate, and you lose the ability to claim most business expenses. Most contractors see their take-home fall by 20–30% compared to outside IR35 at the same gross rate.

How is IR35 status determined?

HMRC looks at the substance of the working arrangement, not just what the contract says. The key tests are: substitution (can you send someone else to do the work?), control (does the client control how and when you work?), and mutuality of obligation (is the client obliged to offer work, and are you obliged to accept it?).

Since April 2021, medium and large private sector companies must determine the IR35 status of their contractors using a Status Determination Statement (SDS). Small companies (under the Companies Act threshold) are exempt, meaning the contractor determines their own status.

What's the most tax-efficient structure outside IR35?

The standard approach is to draw a salary up to the personal allowance (£12,570 for 2024/25) or the NI Secondary threshold, then take remaining profit as dividends. Dividends attract a lower rate of tax — 8.75% in the basic rate band, 33.75% in the higher rate band — and don't attract National Insurance.

This calculator assumes a salary of £12,570 by default (the optimum for most contractors), but you can adjust the slider to model higher salaries if you prefer the simplicity of PAYE or are using the salary to maximise pension contributions.

If I'm inside IR35, should I increase my day rate?

Yes — and the "equivalent day rate" figure above shows you exactly what to ask for. The additional costs inside IR35 include employer's NI (13.8%), the apprenticeship levy (0.5%), and the loss of the dividend tax advantage. This typically means you need to add 25–35% to your day rate to match the same net take-home outside IR35.

Not all clients will pay this premium, but it's a fair and factual negotiating position: you're simply asking to be made whole for the additional tax burden they're placing on you by determining your role as inside IR35.

Can I claim expenses inside IR35?

Inside IR35, your allowable expenses are severely restricted — essentially to the same things a permanent employee could claim. Travel to a temporary workplace (under 24-month rule) may be allowable in some circumstances, but most typical contractor expenses — home office, equipment, professional subscriptions — are not deductible against your IR35-caught income.

This is one of the less-discussed costs of being inside IR35. Outside IR35, legitimate business expenses reduce your corporation tax bill before you pay yourself. Inside IR35, you bear those costs out of your post-tax income.

What happens if HMRC disagrees with my IR35 status?

If HMRC determines your contract should have been inside IR35, they can raise an assessment for unpaid tax plus interest, going back up to 6 years (or 20 years in cases of fraud or deliberate non-compliance). The financial exposure can be significant — often tens of thousands of pounds.

Since the 2021 off-payroll reforms, liability for status determination errors in medium and large companies sits with the end client or fee-payer, not the contractor. However, if you're working with a small company or are self-determining your status, the risk remains yours. IR35 insurance is available from specialist providers and is worth considering.

Is an umbrella company better than a limited company inside IR35?

For roles that are genuinely inside IR35, an umbrella company is often simpler than running a limited company. The tax outcome is broadly the same, but you avoid the administrative overhead of running a company — no corporation tax returns, no Companies House filings, no accountant fees for company accounts.

The main catch with umbrella companies is margin: reputable ones charge £15–£25/week. Mini umbrella schemes (which artificially split employment across multiple companies) are HMRC-targeted tax avoidance arrangements and should be avoided entirely — contractors can be held liable for unpaid tax even if they weren't aware of the scheme's structure.
